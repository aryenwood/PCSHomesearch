Neighborhoods Off-Post
Watertown is the main hub with the most amenities and home selection. Evans Mills sits closest to the gates and is the top choice for families wanting a short commute. Carthage and Black River offer lower prices. Lowville is scenic and quiet, about 30 min out.

The Winter Reality
Fort Drum gets 100–180 inches of snow annually — one of the snowiest bases in the US. Budget for a 4WD vehicle, quality winter gear, and heating costs. Natural gas is cheapest; avoid oil/kerosene. The NY HEAP program can help with utility bills.
Avg snowfall: 140 in/year

Schools
On-post housing feeds into Indian River CSD or Carthage CSD depending on your neighborhood. Off-post, General Brown CSD (Evans Mills/Dexter area) is highly rated. Watertown City SD serves most of the city. Contact the Fort Drum School Liaison at (315) 772-3214.
Top rated: General Brown CSD
Cost of Living
Watertown is very affordable by national standards. Median home prices around $185k. Groceries and dining are reasonable. BAH goes further here than at most bases — many families pocket money after housing. Factor in heating costs during winter months.
Median home price: ~$185k
Things to Do
Thousand Islands is stunning in summer — boating, fishing, camping. Adirondack Park is under 2 hours. Tug Hill for snowmobiling. Ottawa and Montreal are 2–3 hours for long weekends. Syracuse is 90 min south for concerts and city amenities.
Closest city: Syracuse, 90 min
Getting Around
A car is essential — 4WD highly recommended. Multiple base gates; your unit's location matters for commute. Most off-post neighborhoods are 5–20 minutes from the main gate. Nearest major airport is Syracuse Hancock (SYR), about 90 min south.

Frequently Asked Questions

Real answers to the questions every Fort Drum family asks.

Pulled from real searches, military spouse groups, and years of working with families PCSing to Drum.

Should I buy or rent at Fort Drum?+
Fort Drum is one of the best bases in the Army to buy a home. Home prices are well below the national average (median ~$185k) and many soldiers find their VA mortgage payment comes in under their BAH — meaning they're building equity while living essentially for free. The Watertown rental market is also strong, so if you PCS in 3 years you can convert it to a rental rather than selling. If you have less than 2 years on your contract or significant uncertainty, renting may make more sense. Talk to Joseph for a personalized recommendation based on your specific situation.
How bad are the winters, really?+
Honest answer: they are bad. Fort Drum sits in the Lake Ontario snow belt and receives 100–180 inches of snow annually — temperatures regularly hit -20°F. A 4WD or AWD vehicle is not optional, it's a necessity. Budget for quality winter gear for the whole family and higher heating costs (natural gas is cheapest; avoid oil). That said, families who embrace it love the snowmobiling, skiing, ice fishing, and tight-knit community that the isolation creates. Come prepared and you'll be fine.
What's the on-post housing waitlist like?+
On-post housing is managed by Mountain Community Homes (FDMCH) and waitlists typically run 6–18 months, especially for larger homes and during peak PCS season (summer). Apply as early as possible at fortdrummch.com or call (315) 955-6644. Most families plan for off-post housing and move on-post when a slot opens. If you decline an offer twice, you go back to the bottom of the list — so only apply when you're ready to commit.
Which school district is best for my kids?+
General Brown CSD (Evans Mills/Dexter/Brownville area) is consistently the highest-rated district near Drum. Indian River CSD serves most on-post housing (Adirondack Creek, Crescent Woods, Monument Ridge). Carthage CSD serves Rhicard Hills on-post. Watertown City SD serves most of the city. Your school district is determined by your address. Contact the Fort Drum School Liaison at (315) 772-3214 for personalized help. All districts provide busing for military neighborhoods.
What neighborhood should I live in?+
Evans Mills is the closest off-post community to the main gate (4–5 min) and extremely popular with families who want a short commute. Watertown has the most amenities — restaurants, shopping, Samaritan Medical Center — and the widest home selection. Carthage and Black River offer cheaper housing but fewer conveniences. Lowville is peaceful and scenic but about 30 min out. Your unit's specific gate location also matters — ask Joseph which neighborhood is best for your brigade and commute.
Can I use my VA loan if I've already used it before?+
Yes — your VA loan benefit is reusable, even if you still have an existing VA loan on another property. You have "remaining entitlement" or "bonus entitlement" that can cover a second purchase. Since Fort Drum home prices are well under the conforming loan limit, most soldiers can still purchase with $0 down even on their second VA loan. Talk to one of our preferred lenders to pull your Certificate of Eligibility and see exactly where your entitlement stands before you start searching.
What does my BAH actually cover in Watertown?+
Your BAH is set to cover the median cost of housing for your rank and dependency status in the local market. In Watertown, BAH goes further than almost any other Army base. For an E5 with dependents ($1,665/mo), a typical mortgage on a $185k home runs $1,100–$1,300/month — leaving real money in your pocket. If you live on-post, BAH covers rent, utilities, and insurance entirely. Off-post, you're responsible for utilities on top of your housing payment — budget an extra $150–300/month in winter.
What do I need to know about heating costs?+
Heating is a real budget line item at Fort Drum — winters are long and cold. Natural gas is the most economical option, followed by electric, then propane. Oil and kerosene are the most expensive — avoid if possible. When house-hunting, always ask about the heating source. Budget an extra $150–300/month in winter months. The New York State HEAP program can help with utility bills — contact Army Community Service at (315) 772-6557 for more information and eligibility.
How do I in-process at Fort Drum?+
All soldiers must report to the Mountain Reception Company (MRC) at Clark Hall, Bldg. 10720, no later than 2359 on the day their leave ends or by their report date. Bring DA Form 31, assignment orders, health/dental records, and your MPRJ. You'll update DD Form 93 and SGLI on arrival. The Housing Services Office (HSO) is also in Bldg. 10720, Room A2-54 — visit them the same day to start your housing search. See the official Fort Drum newcomers page for the full checklist.
Can I bring my pets? How hard is finding pet-friendly housing?+
Pets are welcome off-post — but finding pet-friendly rentals can be challenging, especially with large dogs or multiple pets. Don't bring pets on your first house-hunting trip until you've confirmed a pet-friendly property. On-post housing allows pets with restrictions; contact FDMCH directly for current policies and fees. When buying, pet ownership is never an issue — another strong argument for buying over renting at Drum if you have pets.
What resources are available for military spouses and families?+
Fort Drum has excellent family support through Army Community Service (ACS) at the Family Resource Center, 11042 Mt. Belvedere Blvd, (315) 772-6557. Key resources include: Relocation Readiness Program, financial counseling, the Lending Closet (borrow household items during your move), EFMP for special needs families, and spouse employment assistance. The School Liaison Officer at (315) 772-3214 is invaluable for school transitions. The 10th Mountain community is known for being tight-knit — spouse groups and FRGs are very active here.
Is Fort Drum a good duty station overall?+
Honest answer: it depends on you. The pros: tight community, incredible outdoor recreation, affordable housing where your BAH actually covers your mortgage, strong mission with the 10th Mountain Division. The cons: brutal winters, geographic isolation, limited civilian job market for spouses, limited nightlife. Families who embrace outdoor life, community, and adventure tend to love it. Families who need warm weather, city access, or strong civilian career paths tend to struggle. Come prepared with the right expectations and most families leave with great memories.

Your 2026 VA Loan Guide for Fort Drum
VA loan limits just went up — here's what that means for you.
By Joseph Murtha · March 2026

If you're PCSing to Fort Drum this year, your VA loan benefit is stronger than it's ever been. Here's what changed for 2026 and how to use it.

First, the big one: if you have full VA entitlement — meaning you've never used your benefit, or you've paid off a previous VA loan — there is no loan limit. Zero. The VA will guarantee whatever a lender approves you for, with $0 down. That's not a typo. No down payment. No PMI. Just your Certificate of Eligibility and a lender who knows what they're doing.

For soldiers who already have an active VA loan, the baseline limit for 2026 is $832,750 — up from $806,500 in 2025. That's a 3.3% increase that puts more buying power in your hands.

Here's the math that matters at Fort Drum: median home prices in the Watertown area run well below the national average. We're talking $126,000–$200,000 for solid family homes. Your BAH for 2026 increased 4.9% over last year. As an E-6 with dependents, you're looking at $1,893/month in housing allowance. That covers the mortgage on most homes near post — which means you could be building equity instead of paying someone else's mortgage.

The VA funding fee for first-time use with $0 down is 2.15% of the loan amount. On a $175,000 home that's about $3,762 — usually rolled into the loan so you pay nothing out of pocket at closing. Compare that to a conventional loan requiring a 20% down payment — $35,000 cash — and the VA benefit starts to look like exactly what it is: one of the best financial tools available to anyone in uniform.

One thing I see soldiers miss: get your COE (Certificate of Eligibility) before you start house hunting. It takes 10 minutes online at VA.gov and it tells you exactly what you're working with. Don't wait until you find the right house to figure this out.

If you have questions about how your specific rank and situation plays into this, reach out. I run these numbers every day for Fort Drum soldiers and I'm happy to walk you through it.

Fort Drum BAH 2026: Why Buying Beats Renting Right Now
Your BAH went up 4.9%. Here's how to make it work for you — not your landlord.
By Joseph Murtha · March 2026

BAH rates at Fort Drum just went up 4.9% for 2026. Most soldiers take that money and hand it straight to a landlord. Here's why that's worth reconsidering.

The 2026 BAH rates for the Fort Drum/Watertown military housing area break down like this: an E-5 with dependents receives $1,665/month. E-6 with dependents: $1,893. E-7 with dependents: $2,103. That's your housing budget — tax-free money that doesn't touch your base pay.

Now look at the local market. The median home purchase price in the Watertown area is around $126,000. Even factoring in a modest bump over the past year, you're looking at homes in the $150,000–$200,000 range that cover everything a military family needs — 3 bedrooms, a yard, close to post. At current rates on a VA loan, a $175,000 home runs roughly $1,100–$1,300/month depending on your rate and taxes.

The math: your BAH covers the mortgage and then some. The difference stays in your pocket. And when you PCS out of Drum in 2–3 years, you can either sell the home and walk away with equity, or keep it as a rental property — which Fort Drum's rental market can absolutely support given the constant rotation of incoming soldiers.

The biggest objection I hear: "I don't know how long I'll be here." That's the VA loan's secret weapon. Because you put $0 down, your risk exposure is minimal. In the worst case scenario — you PCS earlier than expected and the market dips — you can rent it out and cover the mortgage without losing money every month.

PCSing to Fort Drum: What Nobody Tells You About Housing Near the 10th Mountain Division
The real neighborhoods, the winter reality, and how to find a home before you get your orders.
By Joseph Murtha · February 2026

Every year I work with soldiers and families PCSing to Fort Drum who had no idea what they were getting into — not because they didn't prepare, but because nobody told them the things that actually matter. Here's what I wish every incoming soldier knew before they started their search.

The neighborhoods that matter: Most families end up in Watertown, Calcium, or Evans Mills. Watertown is the most complete — you have grocery stores, schools, restaurants, and a real downtown. Calcium is tight-knit and popular with military families because of proximity to post. Evans Mills is quiet and affordable. All three are worth looking at depending on your family's priorities.

The winter is real. Fort Drum gets an average of 100+ inches of snow per year — lake effect off Lake Ontario. When soldiers from warmer climates ask me what that means for housing, I tell them: buy close to post. A commute that's 20 minutes in October can be 45 minutes in January. That matters when you're trying to make formation.

Start earlier than you think. The Watertown housing market moves quickly — good homes in the $150,000–$200,000 range don't sit long, especially in spring when PCS season heats up. If you have your orders and you're not actively looking, you're already behind. I work with families who are 60–90 days out and we find better options than the ones who wait until 30 days.

Use your VA benefit. I know I say this all the time, but Fort Drum is one of the best duty stations in the country to buy instead of rent because of the price-to-BAH ratio. You can make your housing allowance actually work for you here instead of just paying someone else's mortgage.

Get a lender approved before you contact a real estate agent. Seriously. Know your numbers — your BAH, your COE status, your credit score — before you fall in love with a house. It makes everything faster and it makes your offer stronger.

I've helped dozens of 10th Mountain Division soldiers and families find the right home before, during, and after PCS season.
